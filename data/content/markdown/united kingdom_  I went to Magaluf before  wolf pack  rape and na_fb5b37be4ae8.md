Cheap prices and the promise of sunshine have drawn British teenagers to Magaluf for decades - but scratch beneath the surface and you will find

a seedy and sometimes criminal underbelly.

‌

Eight men have been sentenced for a total of 73 years after gang-raping a British teenager in a

Magaluf

hotel in 2023. She was so incapacitated she slipped from a semi to a totally unconscious state during the horrifying attack, which was described as including "particularly degrading and humiliating intimidation and violence". At one stage one of the attackers even went into the corridor of the BH Mallorca Resort and offered "free sex" with the

unconscious 18-year-old to passersby.

‌

A dubious nickname of 'Shagaluf' has long been associated with holiday hotspot, and it more than sets the tone: people are visiting for some fun in the sun, no-strings attached. It's a place to go wild. But the advent of the so-called 'manosphere' has introduced a sickening caveat to the hedonism with women seen as 'fair game' - regardless of consent.

The men who have been convicted for attacking the British teenager were not a group of friends, and were not even all known to one another before their sick crime, which they filmed. The horrifying group attack took place on August 14, 2023 and the men have pleaded guilty in exchange for reduced sentences. The five rapists accepted jail terms of nine to 11 years for sexual assault, with three receiving higher prison sentences because they repeated their crimes. The three men who didn't take part in the sex attack but filmed it were handed prison sentences of two years and three months.

The incapacitated teenager was spat on, hit, smacked and repeatedly raped - whilst some of the men laughed and used their phones to film the "several videos focusing on the young woman's private parts in which they appeared forcing her to to carry out sexual acts". All the men were aged between 18 and 26 at the time of the arrests. The victim was found sobbing on the floor of the hotel foyer by a security guard who was arriving for his shift, and swiftly reported the crime to the police.

‌

Investigative journalist Ellie Flynn exposed the casual and implicit rape culture that can be found amongst partygoers on the Magaluf strip in her harrowing and unforgettable documentary for

Channel 4

, 'Magaluf Undercover: Predators and Parties.' The doc followed Ellie and fellow journalist Emily Birtley go undercover for three nights, where they bravely posed as drunk or vulnerable tourists to expose the predatory behaviour on the strip, in clubs, and on the beach.

The results were sinister to say the least. One clip saw Ellie lying slumped on a sun lounger, pretending to be incapacitated, when two men approached her. One said to her: "Are you good? Do you want to talk for a little bit?" When she replies, "I'm good", he continues to bombard her, saying: "You are my last chance, do you want to kiss a little bit?"

Ellie gave him a clear "No" - but he did not leave her alone. Instead, he went to recruit another man, informing him in Spanish, "She's completely wasted," and then saying, "Let's go for it." Another third man then moves beside the undercover journalist on the lounger, and told her, "If you want, I can keep you company."

This interaction "genuinely scared" Ellie, she admitted in the documentary, "I had seen the first two together," she said, "but the sudden appearance of another – and having no idea at the time if they were together – was enough for me to signal security to get me out.

"I leave the beach upset and frightened, feeling like I've experienced an orchestrated attempt to target drunk women alone on a night out in Magaluf. Shockingly, this was not an ­isolated incident, but a pattern that emerged across my three nights in the resort town."

‌

Discussing the horrifying case that saw the eight men sentenced, and the dangers that party resorts like Magaluf can present with the Mirror, Ellie says, "It's just so horrifying, isn't it? I guess first impressions are just, I'm so, so sorry for that girl, what she's been through.

"Having been on these holidays when I was younger myself, and similar places, I really understand some of the problematic culture there. These holidays, unfortunately, can create a breeding ground for this kind of behaviour, because you have young people who are particularly vulnerable, perhaps away from home for the first time, with their friends, trying to have a good time, drinking, trying to party.

‌

"And unfortunately, there are predatory people there who are looking to take advantage of those vulnerabilities. I think what's so shocking about this case - it's not the first one of these horrific group rapes. It says so much, I think, about this toxic culture that eight people got involved with that."

Worryingly, Ellie reveals that when she was working on her documentary, she found that 'a huge percentage' of surveyed men believe that if a woman simply stands near you then "they might be interested in sex".

She added: "It was so shocking. I think there is this assumption that if you are on these holidays, if you're out with your friends having a good time, having a drink, that somehow that that makes you constantly available for sex - even if you're unconscious. [Even if] someone is showing no signs at all that it is what you want, there are people out there who will take advantage of the fact that you were just there".

The reaction from some to her documentary was itself grim and sparked a tide of victim-blaming. "There were people who were replying to me on

Instagram

who were like, 'Well you know, why would you go somewhere like Magaluf if you weren't prepared to experience something like that?' And I think it shows that we have such a long way to go in terms of actually stopping violence against women and girls."

Dr Charlotte Proudman, a barrister and academic who specialises in women's rights, tells the Mirror that the rise of the 'manosphere' championing extreme interpretations of masculinity online has only added fuel to the fire in holiday resorts like Magaluf, and in her view, it's impact can be clearly seen in this shocking gang-rape case. "What we are seeing in places like Magaluf is the collision of toxic online misogyny with a holiday culture of excess, where alcohol, group dynamics and anonymity embolden some men to act with shocking entitlement towards women's bodies," she tells The

Mirror

.

‌

"The influence of the 'manosphere' has normalised the dehumanisation of women and the idea that sexual aggression is a form of male bonding or status," Dr Proudman explains. "The fact that some perpetrators even film these attacks is profoundly disturbing; it shows that for some men, sexual violence is not only committed but performed for an audience for entertainment.

"This is not about lowered inhibitions on holiday, it is about a culture that still allows misogyny and sexual violence to be trivialised, excused and, in some cases, celebrated."

‌

Ellie echoes these sentiments, telling the Mirror that in the age of algorithms creating echo chambers, "We've never seen a further divide between young men and young women and their views and their experiences.

"There was a period in time where I think it felt like things were moving in the right direction," she points out. "You know, we were taking women seriously, and we were listening to their stories and we were saying all the right things to try and combat violence against women and girls and stop this kind of insidious abuse. But at the same time you have the rise of certain influencers and the manosphere and this narrative that young boys are isolated and don't know how to treat girls, and are scared making any approaches. We have these two conversations happening simultaneously and taking people down in completely different directions. I think that is where things are now particularly concerning."

She added that "on one side you have young men" hearing one message and "almost being justified in some cases in the abuse of women and in this mistreatment of women and in degrading women, because there are people, there are high profile figures, who have made them feel like it's okay to do that."

‌

Ellie continued: "It's clear that we need to be having conversations that include both sides of this argument. I have two sons and I don't want them to grow up feeling like they are inherently bad because they are male, that's not true, and I think that somewhere along the way that's a narrative that some young boys have learned to believe, and so things have gone wrong in a sense that things have gone that way, but also ignoring the very real epidemic of violence against women and girls and highlighting those issues isn't the answer. We should be able to do both at the same time."
